1. Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive stats summarize a dataset with a few numbers: where the center is, how spread out the data is, and whether outliers exist.

When to use which?

Use mean + std dev for symmetric data. Use median + IQR when outliers are present. The median is always a safer default for skewed distributions like income or house prices.

2. Probability & Bayes' Theorem

Bayes in Plain English

Start with a belief (prior). See new evidence (likelihood). Update your belief (posterior). The denominator P(B) just normalizes so probabilities sum to 1. This is the foundation of spam filters, medical diagnosis, and Bayesian ML.

3. Key Distributions

Which distribution when?
  • Normal — continuous data, symmetric (heights, test scores, measurement errors)
  • Binomial — count of successes in n independent yes/no trials (coin flips, pass/fail)
  • Poisson — count of events in a fixed interval (calls per hour, defects per batch)

4. Hypothesis Testing

Confidence Intervals & CLT

Central Limit Theorem

No matter the population distribution, the sampling distribution of the mean approaches a normal distribution as sample size n increases (n ≥ 30 is the rule of thumb). This is why so many statistical tests assume normality.

95% CI = x̄ ± 1.96 × (σ / √n)

A 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated this experiment 100 times, about 95 of those intervals would contain the true population parameter. It does NOT mean there's a 95% chance the true value is in this specific interval.

5. Correlation & Regression

Key Formulas
  • r = Σ(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ) / √[Σ(xᵢ−x̄)² × Σ(yᵢ−ȳ)²] — Pearson correlation coefficient
  • R² = 1 − (SS_res / SS_tot) — proportion of variance explained by the model
  • MSE = (1/n) Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² — average squared prediction error
